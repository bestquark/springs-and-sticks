Learning with springs and sticks

Luis Mantilla Calderón1,2 and Alán Aspuru-Guzik1,2,3,4
1 University of Toronto, 2 Vector Institute, 3 Acceleration Consortium, 4 NVIDIA
(2025)
Preprint (PDF) Code arXiv

Motivation

Learning is a physical process, and at a fundamental level is an irreversible process that consumes energy. Our goal in this work is to construct a minimal physical model of learning that makes this energy-learning relation easy to study. We ask:

  • Can a simple mechanical system, built from springs and sticks, approximate arbitrary functions like neural networks do?
  • What does learning look like in such mechanical system?
  • Is there a fundamental, thermodynamic limit that constrains how well this system can learn?

We give answers to these questions and hope to reveal interesting links between physics and machine learning.

How the Model Learns

The dynamics of the springs-and-sticks learner are governed by a Langevin equation, which describes noisy, damped motion toward configurations that minimize the energy:

$$ \frac{d}{dt}\mathbf{x} = \dot{\mathbf{x}}, \qquad \frac{d}{dt}\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{M}^{-1} \big(-\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}U(\mathbf{x},t)\big) - \gamma\,\dot{\mathbf{x}} + \sigma\,\dot{\boldsymbol{\xi}}(t). $$

We compare our mechanical learner to standard neural networks. Despite its simplicity, the model achieves comparable regression accuracy to MLPs. This is because the sticks perform a piecewise-linear (trapezoidal) approximation, which is a universal function approximator.

Finally, we analyze the thermodynamics of learning. We find that when the system’s free-energy change saturates due to environmental fluctuations, learning fails — a phenomenon we call the thermodynamic learning barrier.
